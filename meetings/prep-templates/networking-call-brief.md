## What to Ask Them

The goal is genuine curiosity, not a script. But have at least 3-4 strong questions prepared so the conversation doesn't stall.

### Industry Insights
1. "What's the biggest AI challenge your organization is facing right now that the vendor ecosystem isn't solving well?"
2. "How is [their industry] thinking about AI governance differently than other sectors?"
3. "What's changed most about [their function] in the last 12 months because of AI?"

### Company / Team Culture
4. "What drew you to [their company], and what's kept you there?"
5. "How does [their company] think about the build vs. buy decision for AI capabilities?"
6. "What's the AI team structure like? Centralized, embedded, or federated?"

### Career Advice
7. "What do you wish you'd known before stepping into your current role?"
8. "For someone with my background in [your domain], where do you see the most interesting opportunities?"
9. "Who else should I be talking to? I'm always looking to expand the network."

### If They're Senior / Executive
10. "When you evaluate AI leadership candidates, what separates the top 10% from everyone else?"
11. "What's the most common mistake companies make when hiring their first AI leader?"

## The Ask (If Any)

Be direct about what you need. People respect clarity more than implied favors.

### If you're asking for a warm introduction:
> "I noticed [Company X] is building an AI leadership team. I saw [Person Y] in your network. Would you be open to making an introduction? I'm happy to send you a blurb you can forward so it doesn't create any work for you."

### If you're asking for a referral:
> "I'm applying to [Role] at [Company]. If you'd be comfortable referring me, that would mean a lot. I can send you the specific listing and a quick summary of why I think it's a strong fit."

### If you're asking for advice:
> "I'm at a career inflection point. I've got [brief context]. I'd really value your perspective on [specific question]. No ask beyond your honest opinion."

### If there's no specific ask:
That's fine. Not every networking call needs an ask. Sometimes the relationship IS the value. Build the connection. The ask may come later, from either side.

## Follow-Up Plan

| Timeline | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **Same day** | Send a thank-you message (email or LinkedIn). Reference something specific from the conversation. |
| **Within 1 week** | If they mentioned something you can help with, follow through. Send the article, make the intro, share the resource. |
| **Within 1 month** | Engage with their content on LinkedIn. Comment on a post. Share something relevant to what you discussed. |
| **Ongoing** | Add them to your relationship tracking. Note the conversation date, key topics, and any commitments made. |

### Thank-You Template:
> "[Name], really enjoyed our conversation today. Your perspective on [specific topic] was especially useful, particularly the point about [specific insight]. [If you committed to something: I'll send over [X] by end of week.] Let's stay connected, and if there's ever anything I can do on my end, don't hesitate."

---

## Conversation Reminders

- **Listen more than you talk.** The ratio should be 60/40 them. You learn nothing while your mouth is moving.
- **Don't pitch.** You're not selling yourself. You're building a relationship. If they ask about your work, share it. But don't lead with your STAR stories.
- **Take notes immediately after.** The details you forget in 48 hours are the ones that make your follow-up personal instead of generic.
- **Be honest about your situation.** "I'm exploring my next move" is fine. You don't need to pretend you're casually curious.
