### If no competing offers:
> "I'm in active conversations with other companies at the [VP / Director] level. The compensation discussions are in the $[range] area. I'd like to resolve this quickly because your opportunity is my first choice."

### Rules:
- Never bluff. Only reference offers you actually have or conversations that are genuinely advanced.
- Never name the other companies unless strategically necessary.
- Always explain why you prefer THIS company; it gives them a reason to stretch.

## Exact Scripts for Each Scenario

### Scenario 1: Offer at or above your target
Accept gracefully, then push for equity or sign-on:
> "I'm excited about this offer. The base works well. I'd like to discuss the equity component. For a role at this level, I'm seeing [X shares / X% / $X in RSU value] at comparable companies. Can we bring the grant up to [target]?"

### Scenario 2: Offer below floor
Do not accept. Do not express gratitude for a lowball. Buy time, then counter:
> "Thank you for putting this together. I want to review the full package carefully. Can we reconnect tomorrow?"

On the follow-up:
> "I've reviewed the offer and I'm genuinely excited about the role. But the base of $[X] is below where I need to be and below market for this role. Based on Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and comparable postings, the range for a [title] with my background is $[Y] to $[Z]. I'd like to see the base move to $[target]."

### Scenario 3: Offer at floor
Accept the floor, negotiate upward for total comp:
> "The base is a starting point I can work with. I'd like to see it move to $[target], but I'm also open to bridging the gap with sign-on, equity, or a structured review at six months. What flexibility do you have?"

### Scenario 4: They cite "budget constraints" or geographic discount
> "I understand some companies index compensation to location. My output doesn't change by postal code. The value I deliver -- [reference your key capabilities and outcomes] -- those results are the same regardless of location. I'm delivering top-market-quality work at zero relocation cost to you."

### Scenario 5: They counter with a small bump ($5-10K)
> "I appreciate the movement. I want to be straightforward. The gap between $[their number] and $[your number] is meaningful. This isn't about a few thousand dollars. It's about being compensated at market rate for a senior [your domain] leader. Can you get closer to $[target]?"

### Scenario 6: "This is the best we can do" (final offer)
> "I hear you. Let me be direct. At $[X], I'd be taking a step backward from what the market pays for this level of work. If the base truly can't move, can we look at a sign-on bonus to close the gap in year one and build in a structured review at six months?"

If it's still below your floor after all levers:
> "I've really enjoyed this process and I have a lot of respect for what you're building. But I can't accept at this compensation level. It's below what the market is paying for this role and below what I need to make this move. I hope our paths cross again when the timing is better."

## Emotional Anchors

Read these before the call. Internalize them. Do not negotiate from a place of anxiety.

- **You are worth more than $[CURRENT COMP]. You have been for years.**
- [Reference a key project outcome]. You were paid $[CURRENT COMP] for that.
- You have been [your situation: promoted without raises, underpaid relative to market, etc.]. That is their problem, not your market value.
- The market range for your skill set is $[MARKET RANGE]. You are not asking for a favor. You are establishing the terms of a business relationship.
- If this offer doesn't work, another one will. Your pipeline is active. Your skills are in demand. You have options.
- Do not apologize for asking. Do not hedge. Do not say "I was hoping for" or "I was thinking maybe." Say "I'd like to see the base at $X." Period.

## Call Rules

1. **Do this on the phone, not email.** Email gives them time to craft a "no." Phone gives you the ability to read tone and pivot.
2. **Do not accept on the spot.** Even if they meet your target: "I'd like to take a day to review the full package."
3. **Do not apologize for negotiating.** This is a business conversation between professionals.
4. **Name your number with conviction.** No hedging, no "maybe," no "I was hoping."
5. **Get it in writing.** Verbal commitments mean nothing. Every number, every title, every start date, every sign-on goes in the offer letter.
